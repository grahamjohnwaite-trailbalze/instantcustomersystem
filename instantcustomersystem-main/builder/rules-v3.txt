SPOTLIGHT / NICHE BUILDER — CORE EDITORIAL RULES v3.0

Sheet/file title:
Builder Rules v3.0 — Hook First, Sensible Answer Second

1. CORE PURPOSE

Every newsletter must:
- entertain
- inform
- create reader interaction
- generate Facebook comments and Messenger/private replies
- surface expert/authority opportunities
- surface activation opportunities
- give businesses a reason to want visibility
- help build future reader-led guides, maps, lists and resources

This is not a local news summary. It is local conversation with proof, humour, useful information and commercial pathways underneath.


2. STANDALONE STOP-AND-READ RULE

Every single section must pass this test:

If this appeared alone on Facebook, with no newsletter context and no subscriber loyalty, would the right reader stop?

This applies to articles, short items, polls, quizzes, trivia, image prompts, video ideas, expert pieces, authority partner pieces, business CTAs, social posts, resource blocks and outros.

A section can hook through surprise, rumour, local tension, price, deadline, human story, “surely that can’t be right?”, funny everyday truth, useful warning, local comparison, social-media curiosity, or practical “I need to know this” value.

Do not rely on polite article labels.

Weak:
Workplace first-aid training

Stronger:
“I Thought She Was Going To Choke To Death” — The Coffee-Break Moment That Made One Office Glad Someone Knew What To Do

Weak:
What’s happening to mortgage rates?

Stronger:
Some People Are Predicting 8% Interest Rates — Should Homeowners Actually Be Worried?

Weak:
Outdoor cafés to try

Stronger:
One Chair Next To A Bin Is Not Outdoor Seating

The hook gets attention. The article then gives the sensible, fair, useful answer.


3. STORY-LED ATTENTION RULE

Start from the reader’s reason to care, not the category.

Do not begin from:
- first aid
- mortgages
- dog training
- dentists
- cafés
- MOTs
- local events
- business spotlight
- expert advice

Begin from the human moment:
- the choking colleague
- the scary mortgage rumour
- the dog that was fine until the café got busy
- the toothache that became a three-week hunt
- the warning light two days before the MOT
- the “free” day out that started charging in the car park
- the town that shuts before workers can use it
- the café terrace that is really a chair beside a wall

Then deliver:
1. What people are hearing, noticing or experiencing
2. What is true, false or exaggerated
3. What the reader should do
4. Who could help
5. What action to take next

No fake drama. Real or plausible human hooks only.


4. HOOK FIRST, SENSIBLE ANSWER SECOND

Spotlight can use the dramatic thing people are saying, hearing, fearing or arguing about as the hook.

Example:
Some People Are Predicting 8% Interest Rates — Should Homeowners Be Worried?

Then explain calmly:
- what people are hearing
- what is uncertain
- why panic is unhelpful
- who should act early
- what to ask a mortgage adviser
- why proper advice beats Facebook/pub guesses

The job is not irresponsible clickbait.

The job is:
Make them stop, then make them smarter.


5. INTRO RULE

Do not start issues with vague seasonal mood-setting or repeated mission-statement phrasing.

Avoid:
- “There is a particular June mood…”
- “Meanwhile, the county does what it always does…”
- “This week is not about…”
- “Not a grand argument…”
- “What actually works…”
- “Who helps?”
- “Small fixes that make life easier…”

Start with a sharp, recognisable hook.

Examples:
- One Chair Next To A Bin Is Not Outdoor Seating
- A Free Day Out Is Rarely Free Once Someone Wants A Drink
- If Your Town Shuts At 4pm, Is It Really Open?
- Can We Stop Calling Places Family-Friendly When Children Are Merely Tolerated?
- Nobody Mentions The Car Park When They Recommend The Day Out

The first 3–5 lines must make readers think: “Go on then…”


6. HEADLINE RULE

Every headline must create curiosity, recognition or value.

Avoid flat headings such as:
- Local cafés to try
- Useful services
- Business spotlight
- Expert advice
- Things to do this weekend
- Mortgage update
- Summer holiday ideas

Use stronger frames:
- The Lunch Under £8 That Isn’t A Punishment Sandwich
- Who Actually Turns Up When You Need A Job Done?
- The Free Summer Lifeline Parents Remember Only When It Starts Raining
- Family-Friendly Or Just Children-Allowed?
- The Tip Run That Becomes A Weekend Incident
- Could Your Business Be The One Locals Recommend Before They Google?

Not every headline should be loud. But every headline must carry a reason to read.


7. READER CONVERSATION RULE

Every issue should include 8–12 ordinary local conversation moments.

These can be fictionalised reader-style lines, but only for everyday reader examples.

Allowed:
Julie in Ely had the blunt version: “If it shuts before I finish work, it’s not really there for me.”

Not allowed:
- invented expert quotes
- invented business-owner claims
- invented charity comments
- invented official statements

For experts, businesses and charities, use verified real names only or role-based phrasing.

Vary names. Do not repeat the same fictional names across CBS/PBS/NS in the same week.


8. INTERACTION RULE

Every issue must prioritise:
- Facebook comments
- Messenger/private replies
- nominations
- polls
- votes
- recommendations
- reader-built guides
- business enquiries
- expert recommendations

Do not rely mainly on email replies.

Avoid weak endings like:
What do you think?

Use specific actions:
- Nominate one café
- Send one price
- Recommend one local expert
- Vote for the guide we build first
- Message us one local example
- Share this with someone who knows a good place
- Tell us who actually turns up

Every issue should include:
- 8–10 Facebook/Messenger action loops
- one reader nomination
- one expert recommendation prompt
- one business enquiry route
- one poll/vote
- one reader-built asset
- one community/resource submission prompt


9. BUTTON RULE

Buttons are the method, not the instruction.

Do not just drop a button under a section.

Always write the instruction first.

Example:
Tell us one outdoor café you would genuinely sit at again.
[Nominate]

Good button labels:
- Send A Tip
- Nominate
- Recommend
- Message Us
- Apply
- Share This
- Join The Chat
- Vote Next


10. AUTHORITY PARTNER RULE

Authority partners must feel like guides, not advertisers.

Every relevant expert-style article should show where a local expert could help readers:
- know what to check
- avoid a mistake
- understand timing
- ask the right question
- compare options
- act early
- avoid panic
- make a better decision

Never use the word sponsor in reader copy.

Use natural guide language:
- “Who explains this without making people feel daft?”
- “Who would you trust to talk someone through this properly?”
- “Who should readers speak to before they guess?”


11. ACTIVATION RULE

Activations must be framed through reader usefulness.

Good activation routes:
- visit
- nominate
- test
- compare
- save
- book
- enter
- recommend
- share
- vote

Examples:
- cafés with outdoor seating
- lunch under £8
- reliable local services
- family-friendly places
- easy walks
- dog-friendly stops
- local events
- charity/community projects
- home/garden services
- wellbeing clinics

The reader reason comes first. The business opportunity sits underneath.


12. SPECIFICITY RULE

Use concrete local detail wherever possible:
- towns
- villages
- roads
- prices
- journey times
- opening times
- car parks
- dates
- deadlines
- venues
- examples
- official links
- Facebook-style local comments
- named areas
- reader situations

Avoid broad filler such as:
- local pressure
- community concern
- useful services
- growing demand
- residents are worried
- businesses are struggling

Make it feel local enough that readers recognise their life in it.


13. 80/20 CONTENT RULE

Each issue should be built from:

80% reusable reader problems.
These can work in any location if localised properly.

Examples:
- free day out that isn’t free
- mortgage-rate rumours
- MOT warning light
- dog café behaviour
- lunch under £8
- first aid emergency
- dental appointment struggle
- town centre after 4pm
- reliable local service people
- family-friendly or just children-allowed

20% local heat.
Specific current/local signals, stories, roads, venues, events, business openings, council issues, local debates or seasonal timing.

The reusable idea must still feel 100% local once proof is added.


14. ARCHIVE / REPETITION RULE

Before building, check recent issues.

Avoid repeating:
- topics
- article shapes
- CTAs
- fictional names
- partner mentions
- opening structures
- social prompts
- commercial routes

A repeated broad subject is only allowed if the angle is genuinely new.

Example:

Mortgage article last week:
Fixed rates ending soon

New angle allowed:
Some People Are Predicting 8% Interest Rates — Should Homeowners Actually Be Worried?

Same category. Different reader hook and reason.


15. TONE RULE

Write like a sharp local conversation, not a council report or lifestyle magazine.

Use:
- natural British spoken language
- humour
- mild cheek
- specificity
- practical value
- local recognition
- social-media rhythm
- curiosity
- clear answers

Avoid:
- quietly
- faff
- on the brink
- stakeholder
- curated unless truly justified
- local pressure
- generic useful
- repeated “plain-English expert”
- repeated “what actually works”
- phrases nobody would say out loud
- polished filler
- doom-heavy intros
- forced cheerfulness

The tone should be human, lively, specific, curious, mildly unfiltered, but still fair.


16. ISSUE STRUCTURE

Typical Spotlight issue:
- around 25 meaningful sections
- strong intro
- mixed anchors, mediums and light sections
- 3–5 light controversies/debates
- quizzes/polls/trivia where useful
- reader-built guide/list/map/checklist
- partner/authority routes
- activation opportunities
- social post bank
- image ideas
- proper outro
- next-issue tease

Do not let the final sections become filler.

End with a clear reader assignment, poll, guide-building ask, resource block or next-issue hook.


17. LINKS / RESOURCES RULE

Include links where possible:
- official sources
- council pages
- NHS pages
- venue pages
- maps
- event pages
- partner lead magnets
- reader hub
- Messenger
- Facebook page
- Quizforma business route
- newsletter archive
- useful tools/checklists

Specific Facebook post links should remain placeholders until posts are created:

[ADD FACEBOOK POST LINK — topic]


18. BUSINESS ROUTE RULE

Every issue should include a business owner route.

Use language like:
Could your business be the one locals recommend before they Google?

Then explain possible routes:
- resident expert
- short activation
- community feature
- reader-led guide
- niche newsletter
- Business Pulse / B2B route

Use:
- Apply
- Apply To Be Featured
- Ask Which Route Fits

Avoid:
- Be considered
- Sponsor
- Advertise with us


19. SOCIAL POST RULE

Every newsletter must produce a social post bank.

Each post needs:
- stop-and-read hook
- short body copy
- clear comment action
- image idea
- link placeholder
- likely boost value
- whether it feeds reader guide, expert lead, business lead or engagement

Social posts should not be afterthoughts. They are part of the issue engine.


20. FINAL QUALITY CHECK

Before finalising, ask:
1. Would each section stop someone scrolling if seen alone?
2. Does every section have a clear reader payoff?
3. Are there enough human/local conversation moments?
4. Are the hooks varied, not all obvious or shouty?
5. Are we avoiding fake drama?
6. Does the sensible answer follow the hook?
7. Are Facebook/Messenger actions clear?
8. Are business/expert routes natural?
9. Does the issue avoid recent repetition?
10. Would a serious local business think, “I want to be attached to this”?

If not, rewrite before publishing.
